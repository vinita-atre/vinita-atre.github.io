WonScore-Customer Onboarding

Getting customers to the Aha Moment!

Problem and Hypothesis

Our application had low trial to paid conversion rate.

Based on product usage analytics and interviews with our customer support teams, we hypothesized that trial(new) customers

  • Experienced difficulty recognizing product value,
  • Lacked triggers for engagement with the key activities like testing candidates, adding jobs, and application login,
  • Wanted to understand the candidate assessment experience and reports,
  • Needed in-app help and support resources to set up their account and correctly start using the application.

My Role

I was the solo UX designer on a cross-functional team comprised of a developer, a user researcher, a product owner, and a business analyst.
I was responsible for

  • Determining the overall design direction while partnering with the rest of the team on ideation,
  • Evaluating the designs with stakeholders by partnering with the user researcher,
  • Designing the mockups for dev handoff as per the technical and product constraints.

Discovery Research

Customers surveys showed that both trial and paid customers found the application easy to use however, trial customers were unclear about the value proposition of the application. Both cohorts preferred a hands-on approach of trying out a new application themselves to learn about it. Using market research and competitor analysis, we created a list of best practices and experiences for onboarding which provided broader understanding as well as design inspiration.

Key Activities

  • Customer Surveys
  • Market research
  • Competitor Analysis

Define Problem

We defined the user problems and the business problems using the research data, business strategy and product strategy. I collborated with business analysts and product owners as the voice of the user by advocating for user needs and good UX principles.

Key Activities

  • Define user problem
  • Define business problem
  • Hypothesis creation

Ideation

As a team after consuming the research insights, we brainstormed design ideas as sketches, low-fi wireframes, user flows.

We used design sprint activities like crazy-8s and panel sketches to generate large number of green field ideas. Through the team’s voting, we selected a few ideas to explore in-depth and test with users.

Key Activities

  • Crazy 8s
  • Panel Sketches
  • Stakeholder dot voting


Prototyping

Overall we created 6 design concepts following UX principles of user control and freedom, and system visbility.

I organized design review sessions with product managers, user researcher and business analysts to design in an agile and collaborative way. Working closely with user researcher, I prototyped various happy paths and screens to test the assumptions and hypothesis.

Key Activities

  • Interaction Design

  • Wireframing

  • Interactive prototyping

  • Adobe XD

User Testing

Overall, we conducted 2 rounds of external user testing with 5 and 7 users respectively as well as 2 rounds of internal stakeholder testing with 13 and 17 stakeholders respectively.

In round 1, we tested top 3 design concepts from team voting which quickly showed the difference in expected vs actual feature desirability. We iterated out of the 3 designs futher based on the feedback. We did not move forward with 1 out of 3 designs as they did not meet the customer needs.

In round 2, we tested new design concepts along with the updated design concepts which helped us further validate the design concepts

After drawing insights from data using affinity mapping, overall design concept acceptance rating, quadrant analysis, we prioritized our final design concepts.

Key Activities

  • User Research protocol design

  • Remote Prototype testing

  • Silent Feedback Method

  • Affinity Diagraming

Mockup Designs

Based on the inisghts of user testing, product and technical constraints, we scoped the work. Advocating for the user, I communicated with and helped the Business analyst and the Product owner prioritize the work. We decided to design experiences for trial customers to quickly get them started in using the product to reach their aha movement. We presented help resources upfront to support new customers especially. We also decided to design a preview test experience for trial customers to allow them to correctly explore the tests.

Collaborated with the engineering team during development through agile ceremonies and open communication.

How proposed solution solved the problem

Our quickstart timeline concept was liked as a hands-on approach to get users started in using the product to get to their aha moment which increased user engagement and product feature discovery. The quickstart timeline conatined key value delivery activities to get users to the Aha moment. We designed and tested the steps in the timeline, the layouts as well as the copy. The timeline aimed to increase engagement for trial(new) users early on to recognize product value easily and quickly."user-quote"

Our preview assessment experience highlighted the key value adds of the UX and the IO science in the application.

Our homepage design concept gave customers access to their latest job openings, customer account tile, help content and test candidate button all in one place.

This dashboard like homepage provided an easy entrypoint into the application. We improved the experience for the user by frontloading the most important user action testing the candidates through the home page.

Introduced new value proposition tile that highlighted key features of the product to the user.

Created user tile for clearly telling customers the status of their account, CTA to upgrade and account level actions needed like email verification.

All these upfront product experiences gave the trial customers the necessary information in making a positive purchase decision.

Challenges faced, including design concepts that were ultimately not pursued

This was our first design cycle project at Wonderlic and thus design activities and ideation were challenging for the cross-functional team.

During brainstorming, we went through several different design concepts that ultimately did not satisfy the trial customer’s needs.

Our initial designs for the test preview experience were shown to be overloaded with too much information which overwhelmed the users and led them to abandon the preview without gaining any better understanding of the tests. Using the positive feedback, we gained an understanding of the most critical pieces of information which helped us to design a shorter and simpler test preview experience.

Our landing page design concept explaining the product laying out options for self-learning guided paths discouraged and confused the user as they wanted to cut to the chase and just start using the platform. A user said “ Setting up account is enough work! I dont want to do more than required.”

User testing the guided path design concept for interpreting results showed that users did not prefer to explore the application in a long and linear flow. Overall although we had found the existing wizard experiences to be less popular in previous research, we attempted to design better wizards as enhanced guided paths to be more contextual, informative with answers to some of the most commonly asked questions. However the user testing further proved that users really did not care for learning and help before using the application but would much rather use help resources like FAQ and articles for specific problems that may arise. A user said “I prefer self-guided information but dismissable where I have the option of clicking and learning or exiting” We leveraged the positive feedback about the specific information that was helpful in the guided paths and added them as lightweight one-time tooltips in the experience.

Positive Results and much more to do ...

Direct Impact on Conversion

The redesign of the customer onboarding experience has had a positive impact on the engagement with product, value discovery and conversion rate, at the time of writing (2 months since launch). However, we did not see a reduced time for conversion. I believe this to be a behavior change that will reveal over a longer period of time.

  • Number of trial customers who created job openings increased by 9.2%

  • Number of trial customers who tested at least one candidate using WonScore increased by 7.7%

  • Upto 85% increase in conversion in the following months of trial customers to paid customers( multiple additional factors involved)

  • Because we have help resources available upfront on the home page, we have seen a reduced number of calls to the customer support team.

For confidentiality reasons I have omitted the actual values for these metrics

What I learned

Test with real users.

Because of this project, we realized the importance of external user testing as it provided us with contrasting feedback on our interactive guided paths design concepts when compared to the internal stakeholder testing. The interactive guided paths were found be informative and useful by internal stakeholders but undesirable by external stakeholders.

Use proven UX methods but also improvise as needed

I proposed using the talk aloud method for testing prototypes with external users which proved highly valuable to get more specific and genuine feedback. The interviewer was able to follow up with appropriate questions due to the increased interview engagement to uncover deeper insights about the design concepts.

Design and Communicate research insights to all stakeholders

I lead the team to create a hierarchical affinity diagram to view themes in our research data which helped design interactions and concepts, informed product strategy to the product and business stakeholders as well as helped build empathy and understanding of the user for the engineering team.
